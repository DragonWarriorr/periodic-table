Carbon-based organisms

Carbon-based organisms: Life on Earth is known as carbon-based because on Earth, a lot of organisms contain building blocks, consisting primarily of carbon, with a mixture of other elements.

A lot of organisms are carbon-based because carbon can form stable bonds with itself and other elements. This allows the vast variety of complex and large molecules.

Moreover, each carbon atom can form bonds simultaneously with 4 other atoms.

Hydrogen-rich environments and organisms

Hydrogen-rich atmospheres: There is research being done by a scientist named Seager, which suggests that simple organisms may live on exoplanets with hydrogen-rich atmospheres.

Additionally, it is a possibility that Seager’s research has confirmed that carbon-based lifeforms or organisms can survive in hydrogen-rich environments.

Seager also hypothesises that since hydrogen is around 14 times lighter and oxygen and nitrogen and Earth is dominated by oxygen and nitrogen gases: if a hiker were to hike up Mt. Everest, in a hydrogen-rich atmosphere, the hiker should be able to get 14 times higher without running out of air.

Therefore, Seager hypothesises that since hydrogen is lighter, in an atmosphere dominated by hydrogen, the atmosphere will probably be more expansive than a nitrogen and oxygen-rich atmosphere, such as Earth’s.

Phosphorous-based organisms

Phosphorous-based life means a hypothetical extraterrestrial lifeform based on the element phosphorous instead of carbon. It has been suggested as a remote possibility.

Phosphorous has been suggested to be a possible biochemical building block since phosphine (PH3) is a compatible solvent.

However, an issue is the small variety of phosphorous hydrides.

At most, it has been proven that it may be able to form chains with some other elements, such as: nitrogen, carbon and silicon.

Another issue is that phosphorous is not produced as much as other elements, such as hydrogen and carbon.

This is because it is only produced in moderately huge stars (which should be greater than 15 solar masses) by carbon or neon fusion.

A further issue with phosphorous being a potential building block is that a large quantity of phosphorous in the terrestrial lithosphere is in the form of phosphates.

Phosphates are not very soluble and require more processing to be made more available to organisms.

Phosphorous is crucial to terrestrial lifeforms as it forms the backbone of deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) and the energy molecule, ATP.

Silicon-based organisms

Silicon does seem like a plausible alternative to carbon as it is directly below carbon in the periodic table, so its basic chemistry is like carbon, and silicon is also common in the universe.

For example, when carbon reacts with 4 hydrogen atoms to form methane (CH4), silicon forms silane (SiH4).

So, both elements, carbon and silicon can form polymers (long chains), in which they could alternate alongside oxygen./p>

The reason that silicon cannot replace carbon is that:

When carbon is oxidized in respiration, it forms carbon dioxide, which is a waste material that can be easily be removed from an organism’s body.

However, when silicon oxidizes, it forms a solid. (It forms a solid because, immediately after formation, silicon dioxide will make itself into a lattice.)

Therefore, since it forms a solid when oxidized, a silicon-based organism would have major respiratory challenges.
