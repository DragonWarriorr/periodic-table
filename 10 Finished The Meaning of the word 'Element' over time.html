The Meaning of the word 'Element' over time

Definition of the word: ‘element’ for Greeks

•The first known definition of the word, 300BC by Aristotle: ‘element’ = earth, fire, water and air. These four elements formed the basis for alchemy.

•There were some Greeks who thought of wood as an element.For example, it could be cut into smaller and smaller pieces, until it was no longer cuttable. This was one of the first ideas that was similar to atoms.Did you know, the word ‘atom’ comes from the Greek word ‘atomos’, meaning uncuttable? The word ‘atomos’ is from the Greek philosopher, Democritus.

Definitions of the word ‘element’ to alchemists

The most famous aim of alchemy was to transform base materials (e.g., transforming lead into gold). In ancient times, transformations of matter were believed to be changes in the amounts of elements present in the substance. These theories of alchemy were proven false by the scientific method. In fact, transformations of matter are called chemical reactions and we have discovered that in chemical reactions, the number of matter/elements present always stays the same.

However, many basic techniques used by modern chemists were first developed by alchemists in medieval Europe, Ancient China and Islamic Caliphate. (Practical knowledge about matter and their reactions were also found out by apothecaries, glassmakers and metallurgists as well as alchemists.)

A new version of alchemy started around 1500 with salt, mercury and sulfur at its core. The focus of the new version of alchemy was not on their physical properties but on the ‘principles’ of these substances. Importantly, chemists demonstrated that air could not be an element because air is a mixture of gases. Moreover, chemists also showed that water is a compound.

Timeline of the word: ‘element’ after the 1600s

1649

In 1649, the idea of elements changed dramatically as phosphorous was discovered by Henning Brand, who was in search of the Philosopher's Stone.

1799

In 1799, the invention of the electric battery gave chemists a new tool for analysis of chemicals, which was electrolysis (electrical splitting). Many new elements were discovered using electrolysis in the last 3 decades of the 19th century (1800s).

1808

In 1808, John Dalton (an English chemist) proposed that all atoms of a specific element are identical and different from atoms of other elements.

The measurable and crucial difference was the mass of the atoms. For example, hydrogen atoms were the lightest, oxygen heavier, sulfur heavier and iron is even heavier.

This helps explain the fact that compounds are always composed of fixed ratios of substance by mass. (E.g., iron in a sample of iron sulfide usually accounts for 63% of the compound’s mass, no matter the size of the sample.)

1860s

In the 1860s, Robert Bunsen (inventor of the Bunsen Burner) and Gustav Kirchhoff (who were both German scientists), added spectroscopy as a new technique for chemical analysis.

Using the spectroscope (an instrument of their invention), they studied the spectrum of light emitted when specific elements are heated and vaporized.

This led to the discovery of many unknown elements since, when studying the emission spectra of all known elements, they noticed the presence of unfamiliar lines in the spectra of these substances.

Later in time...

•After this, chemists began to realize that the growing list of elements seemed to fit into groups according to their reactions and properties.

•This later led to Demetri Mendeleev’s periodic table.

Currently

•Currently: an element = any substance that can’t be decomposed into simpler substances by ordinary chemical processes.

•It can also be descibed as a pure substance in which all atoms will have the same number of protons.
